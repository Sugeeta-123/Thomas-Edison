Thomas Edison







Thomas Alva Edison was one of the most prolific inventors in history, with over 1,000 patents to his name. Born on February 11, 1847, in Milan, Ohio, Edison was largely self-educated, having only three months of formal schooling. Despite this, his curiosity and tenacity led him to become one of the most influential figures of the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Edison is perhaps best known for inventing the phonograph, which could record and reproduce sound, and the practical electric light bulb, which transformed daily life by providing illumination at night. His work with electricity laid the groundwork for the modern power grid.

In addition to these landmark inventions, Edison contributed to the development of many other technologies, including the motion picture camera and the alkaline storage battery. He also established the world's first industrial research laboratory in Menlo Park, New Jersey, where he and his team worked on numerous projects. Edison's legacy extends beyond his inventions; he played a significant role in shaping the modern world through his entrepreneurial spirit and his efforts to bring new technologies to market. His impact on industries such as telecommunications, entertainment, and energy continues to be felt to this day. Edison passed away on October 18, 1931, leaving behind a legacy of innovation that has inspired generations of inventors and entrepreneurs.
